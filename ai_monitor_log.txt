**Context:** This shows how well your creative content resonates with the audience.

## 5 Universal Recommendations for Reviewing Automated Analysis

### 1. Verify the Source and Data (The "Where")
* **Check the Data Inputs:** Ensure the results are based on the data you intended to analyze. Are the numbers, text, or variables correct?
* **Identify the Sources:** If the analysis cites sources, link to them. If it doesn't, ask the system to trace the origin of the information. Never accept a finding without knowing where the information came from.
* **Look for Data Gaps:** Check if the analysis is missing any critical context or if there are large gaps in the data set that might skew the results.

### 2. Apply Critical Thinking (The "Why")
* **Look for Logical Consistency:** Read the analysis not just for the conclusion, but for the steps taken to reach that conclusion. Do the conclusions logically follow the data?
* **Identify Assumptions:** Automated analysis often makes assumptions. Identify every assumption the model made (e.g., "We assume market stability," or "We assume all entries are valid"). Test if those assumptions hold true in your real-world scenario.
* **Spot Bias:** Be aware of potential biases in the training data or the algorithm itself. Does the result reflect an unfair or skewed perspective?

### 3. Contextualize the Results (The "So What")
* **Relate to the Goal:** Does the analysis actually answer the question you were trying to answer? Sometimes, a technically correct analysis is useless if it doesn't address the business or project objective.
* **Consider the Context:** Bring external, real-world context into the analysis. A numerical result might be accurate, but it may not account for recent events, seasonal changes, or known exceptions that are outside the data set.
* **Assess the Implications:** What does this result *mean* for your next steps? Translate the raw numbers into actionable insights.

### 4. Test for Errors (The "How")
* **Spot for Anomalies:** Look for outliers—results that seem wildly different from the rest of the data. These often point to errors, miscategorization, or unusual events.
* **Cross-Reference:** If possible, manually check a small sample of the data points the model focused on to ensure the model didn't misinterpret specific entries.

### 5. Understand the Limitations (The "What If")
* **Acknowledge Uncertainty:** Automated systems are not perfect. Understand that the results are a probability or a statistical trend, not absolute truth.
* **Know the Scope:** Understand the limitations of the model. For example, if the analysis is based on historical data, it may not predict future, unprecedented events.
